Introduction

Artificial Intelligence (AI) Technology is being employed more and more in industries that require automated decision-making processes like insurance companies, financial services, healthcare, and recruitment (Ferrara, 2024). Machine learning can enhance efficiency and predictive accuracy, but can also have built-in biases from the data used to train the models that can create unequal outcomes for certain groups, specifically protected groups based on age, gender, race, etc. This study analyzes gender-bias of a model for prediction of income using the dataset Adult Census Income and uses a technique for reducing bias in the model. The goal is to show that a responsible AI can not only be predictive but fair, transparent and ethical.

Problem Statement

The technique of predicting income is widely used in the analysis of the applications of employment, in the assessment of credit and in financial planning for organisations. But predictive models learned from historical data can inadvertently be biased, bringing about unfair results for some members of the population (Weerts et al., 2023). Thus, assessing fairness is crucial when designing ethical AI systems and solving the associated problems. The problem in this study is treated as a binary classification problem to predict the income of an individual if it is more than $50,000. Logistic Regression model was chosen as baseline classifier because of interpretability and effectiveness. The main purpose is to measure the fairness of the system from a gender perspective based on one or more fairness metrics and to determine if a mitigation technique can be applied to decrease the gender disparity without compromising system prediction performance.

Dataset Description

The Adult Census Income dataset was used, and is presently available in the UCI Machine Learning repository and is a known benchmark for fairness analysis in the machine learning community. The data are demographic and socioeconomic from the 1994 United States Census including age, education, occupation, marital status, race, sex, hours working per week and annual income. The dataset originally had 32,561 observations and 15 attributes. In the preprocessing stage, missing data are indicated by question mark and are excluded from the analysis, yielding 30,162 completed data sets. The data were then split in training and testing sets at 70:30. The target variable was encoded as a binary outcome, where income ≤ $50,000 was labelled as 0 and income > $50,000 as 1. Gender is chosen as the "protected" attribute to ensure that algorithmic fairness is measured.

Exploratory Data Analysis

Exploratory Data Analysis was performed so as to get an understanding of the data characteristics before modelling. It is crucial to understand data distribution due to imbalances in demographic groups that could affect both predictive performance and fairness outcomes (Jui and Rivas, 2024).


Most of the individuals are in the ≤ $50K income group as shown in figure 1, making it a moderately imbalanced data set. This imbalance can lead to the classification model becoming biased towards the majority class, which can risk misclassifying higher-income individuals.


The gender balance indicates that the data set is largely made up of male cases as compared to the numbers of female cases. Data imbalance is a significant ethical issue, which is because machine learning systems tend to work better for over-represented groups in the training data (Rabonato and Berton, 2025).


The distribution by race shows the overwhelming majority of the observations are made of White, a highly representative group, while other racial groups are relatively underrepresented. While this study did not use the attribute "race" as a protected characteristic, the unbalanced demographic observed underscores the need to consider such demographics before designing predictive models.


However, when looking at the gender-income relationship, men are significantly more likely than women to be in the income category of more than $50,000. Such differences can be traced back to the censuses themselves, and a predictive model based on these data potentially learns patterns of difference instead of the real predictive relationship.


Across the racial groups there are also differences in income occurring, with higher income observations clustered mostly in the White group. The finding again reflects the presence of historical in-equality among the training data sets and hence the need for fairness testing prior to deployment of the predictive models.

The age distribution shows that majority are aged between around 25 years to 50 years indicating the economically active working population. The age range was relatively wide in order to allow for training models with sufficient variation yet allow sufficient representation across the age groups.

Ethical Concerns and Bias Identification

While machine learning models can make objective predictions, they can also carry over any historical biases that exist in the data used to train them. Adult Census Income data is due to its structure, going back to pre-modern times, gender-based inequalities, which may be learned and reproduced in the model, will appear. To measure this, gender was chosen as the attribute to be protected in the Fairlearn framework. The baseline Logistic Regression model had an accuracy of 84.26% overall, though the difference in performance between male and female participants was noted in fairness analysis. Based on the Demographic Parity Difference of 0.1444, there is a definite difference in the number of positive predictions between men and women, suggesting gender-biased error despite the model's high predictive accuracy.
 
Fairness Mitigation Approach

A post-processing technique, called ThresholdOptimizer, has been applied in the framework Fairlearn to mitigate observed bias. This approach encourages the predictors to take different thresholds in order to maximize demographic parity while guaranteeing acceptable predictive performance for each protected class. Additionally, it was determined that a post-processing technique would be used, which would not necessitate any changes to the original data or would change the classification algorithm, thus accommodating current machine learning systems that were already available.

Results and Discussion

The overall classification accuracy presented a significant drop from 84.26% to 79.98% after applying ThresholdOptimizer, a common drawback of most classification models, which is that accuracy and fairness are linked by trade-offs. The Demographic Parity Difference, however, fell sharply from 0.1444 to 0.0025, showing that gender disparity is virtually gone. On the other hand, the change in the Equalized Odds Difference was from 0.0529 to 0.1084, indicating that enhancing one fairness metric could negatively impact another. The results indicate that fairness optimization is not a one-dimensional problem of maximizing one indicator of fairness.

Overall Discussion

The results of the developed pipeline show that extremely precise machine learning models are not necessarily fair. While the base Logistic Regression model was predictive, evaluations of fairness showed a great gender disparity that raises the possibility of decision-making that could be discriminatory in real organisational settings. The use of the implemented ThresholdOptimizer achieved a success in decreasing the demographic bias and a slight decrease in the classification accuracy, demonstrating a tradeoff between fairness and predictive ability (Raftopoulos et al., 2025). Organisations should consider several fairness metrics, along with the traditional performance metrics, prior to implementing AI systems in sensitive applications, as these results highlight.

Conclusion

The results of this study successfully verified identification and mitigation of algorithmic bias by applying the Adult Census Income data set in an income prediction model. The Logistic Regression model performed the following tests with good prediction rate but with gender imbalance and measurable levels according to the demographic parity metrics. The main advantages of the ThresholdOptimizer were a slight loss in predictive performance, but a considerable reduction of the demographic differences in favor of becoming more fair. In summary, the results underscore the need for responsible design of AI models, highlighting the importance of considering ethical implications and fostering transparency and equity in automated decision-making.differences in favor of becoming more fair.
